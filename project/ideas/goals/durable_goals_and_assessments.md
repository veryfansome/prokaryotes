# Durable Goals And Assessments

## Core idea

Separate the durable identity of a goal from the current interpretation of its state.

## Reframed for agent-internal goals

For internal agent goals, the durable part is the commitment itself:

- keep the conversation coherent
- finish a promised follow-up
- recover from a failed tool attempt
- verify an uncertain claim before stating it strongly

The assessment is the current read on that commitment:

- healthy or degraded
- satisfied or still open
- well-tracked or stale
- high-confidence or low-confidence

## Why reusable

This prevents a bad collapse where "this goal exists" immediately becomes "this goal should dominate behavior right now." A stable goal and a transient assessment are different things.

## Research roots

- [Rao, A. S., & Georgeff, M. P. (1995). *BDI Agents: From Theory to Practice*.](https://aaai.org/papers/icmas95-042-bdi-agents-from-theory-to-practice/) Retained here for the separation between durable goals, beliefs about current state, and short-lived adopted intentions.
- [Bratman, M. E. (1987). *Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason*.](https://web.stanford.edu/group/cslipublications/cslipublicationssite/1575861925.shtml) Retained here for the idea that commitments persist and can be revised without collapsing into every current action.
- [Carver, C. S., & Scheier, M. F. (1982). *Control theory: A useful conceptual framework for personality-social, clinical, and health psychology*.](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.92.1.111) Retained here for the assessment layer's emphasis on observed state, discrepancy, confidence, and corrective pressure.
